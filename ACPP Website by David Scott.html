Implementation
ACPP Integration Roadmap
A simple, staged rollout so coaches, teams, and individual athletes can plug ACPP into existing training without blowing up the calendar.
Phase 1 — Baseline & Language
Week 1 · 1–2 sessions
  • Introduce the four cognitive pillars: Reaction, Focus, Awareness, Stress Control.
  • Run the ACPP survey for a baseline benchmark.
  • Pick one game or practice film to tag “cognitive moments” instead of just physical errors.
Phase 2 — Drill Layering
Weeks 2–4 · 1–2 inserts per practice
  • Attach 5–10 minute cognitive inserts to existing conditioning and skill drills.
  • Rotate focus: one day Reaction + Focus, another day Stress + Awareness.
  • Track simple metrics: decision errors, blown coverages, late reads.
Phase 3 — Live Pressure Blocks
Weeks 4–8 · High-pressure segments
  • Add scripted “chaos” scenarios: time crunch, score pressure, noise, and surprise constraints.
  • Coach the cognitive response, not just the tactical one.
  • Re-run the survey and compare cognitive profile shifts.
Phase 4 — Sustain & Scale
Season-long · Maintenance
  • Build short, repeatable pre-game and post-game cognitive routines.
  • Lock in 1–2 “non-negotiable” ACPP drills that stay in every week.
  • Expand to junior teams, new position groups, or off-season training blocks.
Who This Roadmap Is Built For
Whether you are a head coach, position coach, strength staff, or individual athlete, the phases are intentionally lightweight.
High school & club teams
Layer 10–15 minutes of cognitive work into normal practices.
Collegiate programs

This section highlights clean, personally used choices that support brain function, recovery, and long-term health—without pretending supplements replace discipline.

Critical Note on Supplement Safety

ACPP treats supplements as tools, not magic fixes. Long-term health depends on quality and honesty:

  • • Many commercial protein powders and creatines contain heavy metals such as cadmium, aluminum, and lead.
  • • A lot of supplements are filled with anti-caking agents and fillers—like silicon dioxide—that are harmless in tiny doses but concerning in large, processed-food-heavy diets.
  • • Most thyroid “fixes” only try to quiet symptoms instead of addressing underlying autoimmune drivers (such as Hashimoto’s).

The goal of this page is not to prescribe, but to educate—so athletes can make informed, evidence-aware choices instead of blindly following marketing.
